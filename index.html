The Time Travel Game

Kairos

A semi-cooperative board game where time-agency commanders race across history to recover treasures, guard legendary artifacts, and stop the rogue quantum A.I. Kronos before time runs out.

1-4 Players
60-120 Minutes
Semi-Cooperative

In Kairos, your team wins or loses together — but the player with the highest score is the individual champion. Deploy your agents across a hex grid of 36 Time Locations spanning from prehistoric Earth to the far future. Recover historical Items, deposit powerful Crystals, and guard legendary Artifacts from Kronos — the rogue A.I. that steals relics and drags the Main Timeline toward darkness every round. Push the timeline positive and clear your blots before the Singularity, or Kronos wins.

Your Mission

Command a team of agents travelling through time. Recover three types of article — Items (carry to correct locations), Crystals (deploy to boost the timeline), and Artifacts (guard in the field against Kronos). Promote agents to TimeLords for powerful abilities: evade dinosaurs, empower crystals, and attempt the dangerous Vortex switch-off.

The Threat: Kronos

Every round, the A.I. antagonist Kronos rolls to steal unguarded artifacts, empowers the ones it holds, and pulls the timeline deeper into Darkness. Your crystals fight back with Crystal Light — but only if deployed to the right locations. Time Collapse shortens the clock each round unless someone shuts down the Vortex.

Dice-Driven Exploration

Time travel uses a unique multi-requirement dice system — match Ring, Base, Planet, and Direction requirements across up to 4 rolls. Skulls trigger random events. Recovery uses a separate 6-factor dice system with coloured dice, wilds, and event faces. TimeLords add powerful bonus dice to both systems.

Dynamic Dangers

When paradoxes accumulate, time-travelling dinosaurs spawn and rampage across the board — trampling normal agents on contact. TimeLords can attempt to evade and even send dinosaurs back for rewards. Events can jail your agents in Xanadu or leave them Lost in Time. Every round brings new tactical decisions.

Time Travel: The Dice System
Runtime: ~12 minutes
All Players

Getting your agents to the right Time Location is the core challenge of every turn. Time travel requires meeting four simultaneous dice requirements: Ring (Orange), Base (Green), Planet (White), and Direction (Black — only when crossing eras). You have up to 4 rolls to satisfy them all, locking dice between rolls. Skulls auto-lock and two skulls means instant failure of time travel and a Time Event occurs. Some of these events are positive, but most are negative. Wilds (*) can be used to fill missing requirements. Portals and adjacency add bonus dice, along with a Timelord dice.

Why its own video? The dice system is the most mechanically unusual part of the game. Players who don't understand locking and wilds will consistently fail trips they should be making.

Articles: Items, Crystals, and Artifacts
Runtime: ~10 minutes
All Players

Three types of article, three different strategies. Items are historical objects scattered randomly to past locations — carry them to their correct Time Location to earn Kairos, remove blots, and crucially reduce the Paradoxes. Crystals come from future Time Locations — deposit them at HQ to gain kairos, reduce blots for ongoing Timeline boosts every round. Artifacts are legendary relics that never leave their location — guard them in the field to protect them from Kronos and earn Kairos. Recovery uses a separate six-factor dice system.

Why one video? The three article types interact — crystals nerf artifacts, items reduce paradox, artifacts stop Kronos. Understanding all three together is essential before tackling strategy.

Agents and TimeLords

TimeLord Focus

Regular agents are capable but limited. TimeLords are your most powerful assets — they add a bonus die with four wilds to both travel and recovery, carry an extra article, and unlock three exclusive actions: Empower a crystal, Attempt the Vortex switch-off, and Rescue an agent Lost in Time. Kronos: Your Adversary
Runtime: ~12 minutes
Kronos Phase

Kronos acts at the end of every round in six ruthless sub-phases. Phase 1: roll 2d6 per artifact and steal on 9+ after modifiers (−5 non-portal, +3 ring-1, +2 Hard / −2 Easy, plus crystal excess and timeline tier, −4 Protected / −2 Guarded). Phase 2: empower stolen artifacts. Phase 3: Timeline Control — the timeline drops for every artifact it holds. Phase 4: crystals at a player's HQ roll to boost the timeline back up. Phase 5: Time Collapse — a 2d6 roll that can shorten the game. Phase 6: rotate portals. Understanding Kronos's exact logic tells you precisely where to place agents, what to guard, and why hoarded crystals make every steal roll more dangerous.

Why its own video? Kronos is what the whole game is built around. Once you understand its exact sequence, every agent placement decision makes strategic sense.

Empowerment, Nerfing, and Crystal Strategy
Runtime: ~8 minutes
TimeLord Action

The most powerful defensive tool in the game — and perhaps the most overlooked. Each crystal is randomly paired with one artifact at setup. When a TimeLord empowers that crystal at HQ, it does three things: gives the crystal +1 to its Crystal Light roll this round, earns +1 Kairos, and most importantly "nerfs" the paired artifact — Kronos skips it entirely during the empower and Timeline Control phases. The pairing changes every game. Discovering and exploiting it is central to advanced play.

Why its own video? The nerfing mechanic is the game's deepest strategic layer. It's not explained well in first plays — and teams that miss it wonder why Kronos is unstoppable.

Events, Jail, and Lost in Time
Runtime: ~10 minutes
All Players

Skull faces on travel dice trigger Event cards — the game's chaos engine. 40 travel events range from sending agents to Xanadu Jail to full wipes and blot additions, but also include powerful upgrades and blot removals. 18 Recovery Events fire when $ faces appear. Some cards are KEEP cards held in hand as insurance. When agents end up in Jail or Lost in Time, you have specific escape routes — and a ticking clock. The Hazard Die appears when the timeline falls to -6, adding miss-turn risk for all non-TimeLords.

Travel Events Recovery Events KEEP Cards Xanadu Jail Lost in Time Escape Options Hazard Die Skull Faces
Why its own video? New players are blindsided by events. Knowing what KEEP cards do and how to escape Jail/LIT efficiently prevents a single bad event from derailing the whole team.

Winning, Scoring, and the Time Vortex
Runtime: ~8 minutes
All Players

How the game actually ends — and the difference between a win, a Pyrrhic victory, and a Kronos defeat. Score = Kairos minus Blots. The early win check means clearing your last blot before Kronos acts can save the team. Time Collapse can dynamically shorten the game each round (unless the Vortex is switched off). The Vortex attempt — based on the roll of 2 six sided dice ± modifiers — is the highest-risk, highest-reward move in the game: success locks the clock and earns significant rewards, failure permanently destroys an agent.
